Ironman Distance Triathlons

Standard Ironman Distance

  • Swim: 2.4 Mi
  • Bike: 112 Mi
  • Run: 26.2 Mi

The Ironman Triathlon is a grueling distance race populated by persistent athletes aiming simply to survive to the finish line. Okay, that’s a bit dramatic – but it’s an intense race.

For a race of this caliber, expect to spend around 36 weeks preparing with periodized triathlon training. On top of the training, you’ll want to adhere to a strict diet and nutrition parameters. One of the greatest resources you can have to help you prepare for an Ironman is joining one or multiple triathlon, running, swimming, and/or cycling clubs. Getting a triathlon coach isn’t a bad idea, either.

Going the Distance

You don’t want to tackle the full distances of an Ironman all at one time except for on race day. In order for your body to handle these distances, you must focus on the individual distances of each sport and progressively increase the mileage over the course of your training.

Selecting a Race

There are roughly 15 Ironman races in North America each year. In order to have enough room to complete the training, you will want to select a race at least 36 weeks in advance of race day. Planning early will give you more options in regards to location.

Nutrition

Many aspiring triathletes forget that there are more parts to the Ironman than just excellent athleticism and fitness. The training also includes a rigorous diet and nutrition requirements – so even when you’re not on the trails or in the pool, you’re still “training” in some way. On race day, the average triathlete consumes up to 8,000 – 10,000 calories. It’s imperative that, on the day of the Ironman Challenge, these calories are comprised of healthy, fueling foods. (Think lean protein and complex carbs – not sugary snacks and energy bars.

Tools of Trade

The equipment needed to finish an Ironman depends on the individual athlete. However, at the most basic level: every triathlete is going to need a swim cap, road bicycle and helmet, and running shoes. Generally, gear for beginners tends to offer more support, while advanced gear involves less weight (for faster results).
